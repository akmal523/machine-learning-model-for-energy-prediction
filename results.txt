WHAT IS BEING PREDICTED
------------------------
Energy per atom (eV/atom) is the total DFT-calculated formation or cohesive energy
of a material divided by the number of atoms in the unit cell.  It is one of the
most fundamental properties in computational materials science: it determines
thermodynamic stability, drives phase-diagram construction, and correlates with
hardness, melting point, and reactivity.

Accurate ML prediction of this quantity allows rapid pre-screening of thousands
of hypothetical structures before expensive DFT calculations are run.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

METRICS EXPLAINED
------------------
Three standard regression metrics are reported for every model.

  MAE  (Mean Absolute Error)
       Average absolute difference between prediction and true value.
       Unit: eV/atom.  Lower is better.
       Interpretation: "On average the model is off by X eV/atom."
       Preferred when all errors matter equally and outliers are not
       disproportionately important.

  RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)
       Square root of the mean squared error.
       Unit: eV/atom.  Lower is better.
       RMSE >= MAE always.  The gap between them indicates how much the model
       struggles with outliers: a large gap means a few large errors are dragging
       the average up.  Penalises large individual errors more heavily than MAE.

  R²   (Coefficient of Determination)
       Proportion of variance in the target explained by the model.
       Dimensionless.  Range: (-inf, 1].  Higher is better.
       R² = 1 → perfect predictions.
       R² = 0 → the model does no better than always predicting the mean.
       R² < 0 → the model is worse than predicting the mean (badly overfit/wrong).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TRAIN / TEST SPLIT
-------------------
  Train set: 80% of samples  (random_state=42 for reproducibility)
  Test set:  20% of samples  — never seen by any model during training or tuning.
  Cross-validation: 5-fold CV used during tuning (scored on MAE).

OBSERVATIONS AND ANALYSIS
--------------------------

1. LINEAR REGRESSION — MAE=0.257, RMSE=0.435, R²=0.727
   The worst performer, as expected.  Energy per atom is a highly non-linear
   function of composition and structure.  Linear models cannot capture
   interactions between features (e.g. between atomic number and lattice parameter).
   R²=0.727 means the model explains only 73% of variance — a reasonable floor
   but far from deployment quality.

2. SVR BASELINE — MAE=0.168, RMSE=0.371, R²=0.801
   A meaningful jump over linear regression.  SVR with an RBF kernel can model
   smooth non-linear boundaries.  However, SVR scales poorly with dataset size
   (O(n²) memory) and is sensitive to feature scale, hence the mandatory
   StandardScaler in its pipeline.

3. GRADIENT BOOSTING BASELINE — MAE=0.179, RMSE=0.284, R²=0.883
   Despite using default hyperparameters, GB significantly outperforms SVR on RMSE
   and R², though MAE is slightly worse.  The lower RMSE vs SVR indicates GB makes
   fewer large errors — important for materials screening where outliers can
   mislead downstream calculations.

4. RANDOM FOREST BASELINE — MAE=0.144, RMSE=0.259, R²=0.903
   Best baseline by a clear margin.  RF's ensemble of deep, decorrelated trees
   captures complex feature interactions naturally.  The RMSE/MAE gap is small
   (0.115), confirming consistently moderate errors without catastrophic outliers.

5. SVR (TUNED) — MAE=0.153, RMSE=0.356, R²=0.817
   Tuning C, gamma, and epsilon improved MAE from 0.168 → 0.153 and R² from
   0.801 → 0.817, but SVR still cannot match tree ensembles on this dataset.
   The likely cause: SVR uses a single kernel for the entire feature space, which
   struggles when the dataset contains both continuous and discrete-like features
   (e.g. n_species is always a small integer while lattice parameters vary
   continuously).

6. GRADIENT BOOSTING (TUNED) — MAE=0.146, RMSE=0.243, R²=0.915
   Tuning learning_rate, n_estimators, max_depth, and subsample produced the
   largest absolute gain for any single model: R² from 0.883 → 0.915 (+3.2 pp),
   RMSE from 0.284 → 0.243 (−14%).  GB benefits strongly from tuning because its
   default learning_rate (0.1) is often too aggressive for datasets with
   noise, and smaller rates with more trees generalise better.

7. RANDOM FOREST (TUNED) — MAE=0.144, RMSE=0.259, R²=0.903
   Tuning produced identical results to the baseline RF on this run.  This is
   plausible: RF's default parameters (n_estimators=100, max_depth=None) are
   already close to optimal for many tabular datasets, and the RandomizedSearch
   with n_iter=10 may not have explored a sufficiently different region of the
   hyperparameter space.  Increasing n_iter would likely yield a marginal gain.

8. STACKING ENSEMBLE — MAE=0.136, RMSE=0.239, R²=0.918   *** BEST MODEL ***
   The stacking meta-learner (Ridge regression) is trained on the out-of-fold
   predictions of RF, GB, and SVR, with the original features passed through
   (passthrough=True).  This gives the meta-model information about when each
   base learner is likely to be correct or wrong, allowing it to up-weight the
   better predictor per region of feature space.

   Key observations:
   - R²=0.918 means the model explains 91.8% of variance in energy per atom.
   - MAE=0.136 eV/atom is a meaningful accuracy for DFT-level pre-screening;
     the typical DFT error budget for energy differences in stable materials is
     ~0.1–0.2 eV/atom, so the ML model is operating within that tolerance.
   - RMSE=0.239 vs MAE=0.136 — the gap (0.103) is moderate, indicating that
     the ensemble still makes occasional larger errors (likely on rare or complex
     compositions underrepresented in the training set).
   - The ensemble outperforms its best individual component (GB Tuned, R²=0.915)
     by 0.003 R² and reduces RMSE by 0.004 eV/atom.  While small in absolute
     terms, this consistent marginal gain across all three metrics confirms the
     ensemble is genuinely capturing complementary signal from the base learners
     rather than overfitting.

FEATURE IMPORTANCE SUMMARY
----------------------------
Feature importances (from the tuned RF and GB) and SHAP values (if shap is
installed) are generated by train_ensemble.py and visualize.py respectively.

In typical materials datasets of this type, the most predictive features are:
  - Cohesive energy proxies (electronegativity differences, valence electron count)
  - Structural features (volume per atom, nearest-neighbour distances)
  - Compositional features (mean atomic number, mean atomic mass)
  - n_species (number of distinct elements) — multi-component alloys tend to
    have lower energy per atom due to mixing entropy effects

SHAP waterfall plots (one per test sample) explain individual predictions:
positive SHAP values push the prediction above the dataset mean; negative values
pull it down.

HOW TO IMPROVE FURTHER
------------------------
  - Increase RandomizedSearchCV n_iter from 10 → 50+ for better coverage.
  - Add matminer featurisers (e.g. ElementProperty, SiteStatsFingerprint) for
    richer compositional descriptors — this typically yields another 2–4 pp R².
  - Try XGBoost or LightGBM as drop-in replacements for GradientBoosting;
    they are faster and often achieve lower RMSE.
  - Use a neural network (e.g. MEGNet, CGCNN) if crystal graph data is
    available — these models routinely reach R² > 0.98 on DFT datasets.
  - Cross-validate the stacking ensemble itself (nested CV) to get an
    unbiased estimate of generalisation error.
